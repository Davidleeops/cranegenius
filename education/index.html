01 / Licensing & Credentials
CERTIFICATION PATHWAYS
OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1427 requires all crane operators on construction sites to hold certification from an accredited testing organization. Here are the primary pathways.
NCCCO Mobile Crane Operator — Telescoping Boom (CCO)
Most Common / OSHA-Accepted / 5-Year Renewal
The most widely held crane certification in the US. Covers hydraulic truck cranes, all-terrain cranes, and rough terrain cranes with telescoping booms. Required on virtually all commercial construction sites. Issued by the National Commission for the Certification of Crane Operators (NCCCO).

NCCCO Tower Crane Operator (CCO-TC)
High-Rise Construction / Luffing & Flat-Top Specialty
Separate certification from mobile crane CCO. Covers hammerhead, luffing jib, and self-erecting tower cranes. Required for all tower crane operators on OSHA-regulated construction sites. Practical exam includes cab entry, pre-operation inspection, and load handling precision tests.

NCCCO Rigger Certification (Levels I & II)
OSHA Required for Rigging Crew Members
OSHA 1926.1404 and 1926.1425 require qualified riggers for crane assembly/disassembly and attaching/detaching loads. NCCCO Rigger Level I covers basic rigging; Level II covers more complex engineered rigging scenarios, multiple crane lifts, and custom rigging configurations.

NCCCO Lift Director Certification
Critical Lifts / Multi-Crane Lifts / OSHA 1926.1431
The lift director is responsible for the overall safety and execution of crane lifts. Required by OSHA 1926.1431 for critical lifts (over 75% capacity, personnel lifts, or lifts over energized lines). One of the highest-value credentials in the industry, typically commanding $75–100+/hr.

CICB — Crane Institute Certifications
OSHA-Accepted NCCCO Alternative / All Crane Types
The Crane Institute Certifications Board (CICB) is ANSI-accredited and OSHA-accepted as an alternative to NCCCO. Covers crane operators, riggers, signalpersons, and lift directors. Some employers prefer CICB; check your employer's requirements before choosing a certification body.

03 / Federal Compliance
OSHA REGULATIONS
Key federal regulations governing crane operation, operator qualification, and job site safety. Understanding which standard applies to your work type is essential.
1926 Subpart CC
Cranes and Derricks in Construction
29 CFR 1926.1400–1926.1442 · Primary construction crane standard
▾

The primary OSHA regulation governing cranes and derricks on construction sites. Enacted in 2010 and updated through 2018, this standard replaced the old 1926.550. Key provisions include mandatory operator certification, assembly/disassembly director requirements, power line safety procedures, and critical lift planning requirements.

  • 1926.1402 — Ground conditions: Operator must be informed of ground bearing capacity; employer responsible for adequate ground support
  • 1926.1403 — Assembly/Disassembly: Must be directed by a qualified A/D director; procedures must follow manufacturer instructions
  • 1926.1407–1408 — Power line safety: Required distances (10 ft for lines up to 50kV), spotter requirements, de-energization procedures
  • 1926.1412 — Inspections: Shift inspections, monthly inspections, and annual inspections required with documented records
  • 1926.1416 — Equipment operating procedures: Pre-shift inspection, operator manual availability, safety devices operational
  • 1926.1425 — Qualified riggers: All rigging must be performed by qualified riggers for A/D and load attachment/detachment
  • 1926.1427 — Operator certification: All operators must be certified by NCCCO, CICB, or employer-audited program (or meet state licensing)
  • 1926.1431 — Hoisting personnel: Strict requirements for personnel lifts including PE-stamped lift plan, tested equipment, qualified operator and rigger
📄 Read Full Regulation on OSHA.gov →
1910.179
Overhead and Gantry Cranes
29 CFR 1910.179 · General industry overhead cranes
▾

Governs overhead cranes (bridge cranes, gantry cranes) in general industry settings such as manufacturing facilities, warehouses, and fabrication shops. This is the general industry standard — different from 1926 Subpart CC which covers construction.

  • Rated load markings plainly marked on each side of the crane
  • All pendant controls must be clearly marked to indicate function and direction of motion
  • Inspection requirements: daily, monthly, and periodic inspections with written records
  • Hoist limit switches required on all hoists; must be tested before each use
  • Operators must be designated and trained; no specific certification required (unlike construction)
  • Maintenance: preventive maintenance program required; records maintained for crane life
📄 Read Full Regulation on OSHA.gov →
1926.1427
Operator Certification / Qualification
The crane operator licensing rule — NCCCO / CICB / State License
▾

This is the specific section of Subpart CC that requires all crane operators on construction sites to be certified. Operators must hold certification from an accredited certification organization (NCCCO or CICB), hold a state or local license where required, or meet the employer-audited program standard. Certification must be crane-type specific.

  • Certification must be specific to the crane type and capacity being operated
  • NCCCO and CICB are the two OSHA-recognized national certification bodies
  • California, New York City, Chicago, and other jurisdictions have additional state/local licensing requirements that must be met in addition to NCCCO
  • Employers using the "employer audited" path must document and audit operator qualifications against established criteria
  • Certification renewal: NCCCO requires renewal every 5 years via recertification exam
  • Temporary operator permits: available in limited circumstances while pursuing certification
📄 Read 1926.1427 on OSHA.gov →
State Licensing
State & Municipal Crane Operator Licensing
California, New York City, Chicago, and other jurisdictions
▾

Several states and municipalities require crane operator licenses in addition to NCCCO certification. These are not substitutes for NCCCO — you typically need both. Key jurisdictions with their own licensing requirements:

  • California (CAL/OSHA) — No state crane license, but CAL/OSHA enforces strict site-specific requirements; tower cranes require additional engineering review
  • New York City (NYC DOB) — Rigorous NYC Crane Operator License (Class A for mobile, Class B for tower) required in addition to NCCCO. Multi-year practical experience required
  • Chicago (City of Chicago) — City of Chicago Crane Operator License required for all work within city limits, in addition to IUOE membership or NCCCO
  • Hawaii — State crane operator certification required; must pass state exam administered through DLIR
  • Connecticut — State certification required for crane operators; administered through CT DOL
  • Nevada — State licensing requirements for crane operators; check with Nevada OSHA
04 / Equipment Standards
ASME B30 STANDARDS
The ASME B30 series covers the design, construction, installation, operation, inspection, testing, and maintenance of cranes, hoists, and related lifting equipment. OSHA regulations frequently reference and adopt B30 standards.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.5 — Mobile and Locomotive Cranes
The primary standard for mobile cranes including truck-mounted, rough terrain, and all-terrain cranes. Covers design, construction, marking, inspection, testing, maintenance, and operation.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.3 — Construction Tower Cranes
Governs tower cranes used in construction. Covers erection, dismantling, climbing, inspection, and operation of hammerhead, luffing jib, and self-erecting tower cranes.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.2 — Overhead and Gantry Cranes
Covers bridge cranes, gantry cranes, and monorail hoists used in general industry. Companion standard to OSHA 1910.179.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.9 — Slings
Covers wire rope, chain, metal mesh, natural fiber rope, synthetic fiber rope, and synthetic web slings. Defines rated loads, inspection criteria, and removal-from-service standards.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.10 — Hooks
Covers hooks used in crane and hoist applications. Includes design requirements, load ratings, inspection intervals, and removal-from-service criteria for swivel hooks, shank hooks, and sorting hooks.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.26 — Rigging Hardware
Covers shackles, links, rings, swivels, turnbuckles, eye bolts, and hoist rings. Sets minimum design factors (5:1) and inspection requirements for all rigging hardware used with cranes.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.20 — Below-the-Hook Lifting Devices
Governs lifting beams, vacuum lifting devices, coil hooks, plate clamps, and other below-the-hook attachments used with cranes. Critical for safe attachment of non-standard loads.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.22 — Articulating Boom Cranes
Covers loader cranes (knuckle boom cranes) and articulating boom cranes mounted on vehicles. Applies to HIAB-type cranes used for material delivery and installation on construction sites.
📐
ASME Standard
B30.23 — Personnel Lifting Systems
Governs the use of cranes to hoist personnel. Sets the performance and safety requirements for personnel platforms, basket hoisting, and fall protection. Must be read alongside OSHA 1926.1431.
05 / Test Preparation
NCCCO EXAM PREP
Know what's on the test before you sit for it. NCCCO written exams cover load charts, rigging, safety regulations, equipment inspection, and math. Here's the breakdown.
Exam Format Topics Covered Fee Pass Rate
CCO Core Written 100 questions · 2.5 hrs Safety, inspections, load charts, rigging basics, site conditions $225 ~55–65%
CCO Telescoping Boom Specialty 60 questions · 1.5 hrs Hydraulic crane mechanics, boom configurations, outrigger setup, load chart math $150 ~60%
CCO Tower Crane Specialty 60 questions · 1.5 hrs Tower crane assembly, electrical systems, radius/capacity, wind loading $150 ~50%
CCO Lattice Boom Crawler 60 questions · 1.5 hrs Crawler setup, boom angles, outrigger equivalents, counterweight $150 ~58%
Rigger Level I 80 questions · 2 hrs Sling types/ratings, hardware, hitch configurations, load weight estimation $200 ~62%
Lift Director 100 questions · 2.5 hrs Critical lift planning, site surveys, multi-crane lifts, OSHA regulations, communication $250 ~48%
Load Chart Math
Most-Failed Topic Area
Load chart questions appear on every CCO exam. You must be able to read manufacturer load charts at various radii, boom angles, and configurations. Practice with real Grove, Liebherr, and Link-Belt load charts before the exam. Key formulas: Net capacity = Gross capacity − deductions (block, headache ball, rigging). Always read the chart for the specific configuration (e.g., fully extended outriggers, specific boom length and angle).
20–25% of Exam Practice with Real Charts
Rigging Math
Sling Angles & Load Ratings
Rigging questions test your ability to calculate sling tensions based on sling angle, determine the correct sling for a given load, and apply the appropriate hitch configuration. Key rule: sling tension increases dramatically as the angle decreases — at 30° from horizontal, sling tension is 2x the load per leg. NCCCO provides a formula reference card during the exam.
15–20% of Exam Formula Card Provided
06 / Find Your Test Site
NCCCO TEST CENTERS
NCCCO written exams are administered at Pearson VUE testing centers nationwide. Practical exams are held at NCCCO-accredited practical examination sites — these are often union training centers, crane company yards, or dedicated testing facilities.
📝
Written Exams
Pearson VUE Testing Centers
NCCCO written exams administered at 500+ Pearson VUE centers in every state. Schedule at pearsonvue.com/nccco. Bring valid government-issued ID and your NCCCO candidate number.
🏗
Practical Exams
NCCCO Accredited Sites
Practical exams held at NCCCO-accredited sites with qualified examiners and appropriate equipment. Use the NCCCO Practical Exam Site Locator at nccco.org to find sites near you. Availability varies by crane type.
📅
Scheduling
How to Register
Register at nccco.org. Create an account, apply for the exam type(s) you want, pay fees, and schedule your written at any Pearson VUE center. Written and practical exams can be taken in any order — both must be passed within 12 months.
Key Testing Deadlines & Rules
✓  Written and practical exams must both be passed within 12 months of each other
✓  Written exams can be retaken after a 30-day waiting period
✓  Bring 2 forms of valid ID to the testing center (one photo ID)
✓  No notes, phones, or reference materials allowed during written exam
✓  NCCCO provides a formula reference card during the practical exam
✓  Results for written exams available immediately on screen

08 / Common Questions
EDUCATION FAQ
Is NCCCO certification required by law?
Yes, on construction sites. OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1427 requires all crane operators on construction sites to be certified by an accredited third-party organization — NCCCO or CICB are the two nationally recognized bodies. Several states and cities (NYC, Chicago, Hawaii) also require additional state or municipal licensing on top of NCCCO. In general industry (manufacturing, warehouses), OSHA 1910.179 requires operators to be "designated and trained" but does not mandate third-party certification — though NCCCO certification is still the industry best practice.
What's the difference between NCCCO and CICB certification?
Both NCCCO and CICB are ANSI-accredited and OSHA-accepted for construction site crane operator certification. NCCCO (National Commission for the Certification of Crane Operators) is more widely recognized nationally — it's the default certification requested by most GCs and crane companies. CICB (Crane Institute Certifications) is a legitimate alternative accepted by OSHA and some employers. If you're unsure which to get, NCCCO is the safer choice for nationwide job market recognition. Check with your employer or union before choosing.
How long does it take to get NCCCO certified from scratch?
With hands-on crane experience, a motivated operator can prepare for and pass the NCCCO CCO written exam in 2–4 months of self-study. The practical exam requires booking a slot at an accredited test site which may have wait times of 1–8 weeks depending on your location. Total time from starting study to holding your CCO card is typically 3–6 months for experienced operators. New operators with no prior crane experience who go through a union apprenticeship or formal training program typically take 6–18 months.
What does the NCCCO practical exam involve?
The CCO Mobile Crane practical exam consists of a pre-operation inspection (identifying items that would take the crane out of service), a crane setup evaluation (outriggers, level, rigging), and a load handling precision test (moving a load through a course without touching markers). The exam uses a real crane at an accredited site — typically a 40-100 ton all-terrain or truck crane. Examinees are scored on safety, precision, and procedure. Bring the NCCCO candidate handbook with scoring criteria to your prep sessions so you know exactly what examiners are looking for.
What is a "qualified rigger" and do I need to be certified?
OSHA 1926.1401 defines a "qualified rigger" as someone with a recognized degree, certificate, or professional standing in rigging, OR who has extensive knowledge, training, and experience sufficient to solve rigging problems. OSHA does not currently mandate specific third-party certification for riggers — but NCCCO Rigger Level I and Level II certifications are widely accepted as meeting the "qualified rigger" standard. Most reputable crane companies and general contractors require NCCCO or CICB rigger certification for their rigging crews. It is the industry best practice and strongly recommended.
What is ASME B30 and does it have the force of law?
ASME B30 is a voluntary consensus standard — it is not a federal law by itself. However, OSHA regulations frequently reference and incorporate B30 standards "by reference," which gives them the force of OSHA law in those specific applications. For example, OSHA 1926.1412 references ASME B30.5 inspection criteria. Additionally, ASME B30 compliance is the industry-accepted standard of care — in a legal dispute or accident investigation, deviations from B30 will be used as evidence of negligence even where not directly required by OSHA.
How do I renew my NCCCO certification?
NCCCO certifications must be renewed every 5 years. Renewal involves passing a written recertification exam (no practical exam required for renewal). You can renew anytime within 12 months before your expiration date. NCCCO will send email reminders. The recertification exam content is the same as the original written exam. Continuing Education Units (CEUs) earned through NCCCO-approved programs can also be submitted in lieu of the recertification exam — check the NCCCO website for current CEU requirements and approved providers.
